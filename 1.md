You are touching on the core of SIMD performance tuning: the trade-off between
Memory Bandwidth (loading precomputed mappings) and ALU Throughput (shuffling in real-time).
The "Load vs. Shuffle" Reality
While loading precomputed mappings seems simpler, shuffling is almost always more efficient for this specific problem. Here is why:

    Register Pressure: To "dump" pre-mapped vectors into registers, you would need to store 14 unique vectors (2 for each of the 7 steps). Loading these from memory/cache every time creates massive pressure on the Load/Store units.
    L1 Cache Latency: A load from L1 cache takes ~4–5 cycles. A shuffle (like vpermd) takes 1–3 cycles.
    Port Contention: Modern CPUs (Intel/AMD) can often execute 2 shuffles per clock cycle, whereas they are limited in how many unique memory addresses they can load simultaneously.

Lane Discipline
You are absolutely correct about the lanes. Since you are treating the 256-bit register as two independent 128-bit lanes (one for
and one for

In AVX2, the instruction _mm256_shuffle_epi32 (the vpshufd opcode) is the "gold standard" for this. It is faster than vpermd because it:

    Operates identically on both 128-bit lanes simultaneously.
    Does not cross the lane boundary (obeying the hardware's natural split).
    Uses an immediate byte (a constant) for the mapping, meaning it doesn't even need a register to hold the mask.

no: a standard shuffle or permute instruction cannot pull a "new" value from a different register while simultaneously rearranging elements from the first.
Most AVX2 shuffles (like vpshufd or vpermd) take one source register and one control mask/immediate to produce one destination.

2. The AVX2 "Blend" Workaround (The most efficient way for you)
Since you are on AVX2, you must use a two-step "Shuffle-then-Blend" approach. This is the standard way to "inject" a value from the original register into your workhorse register. 

    Step 1: Shuffle your workhorse register to align the existing elements.
    Step 2: Blend in the "new" element from the original register using _mm256_blend_epi32 (the vpblendd instruction).

Why Blend is "The Secret Sauce":

    Zero Latency Impact: vpblendd is an "immediate" instruction with a latency of only 1 cycle and massive throughput.
    Selective Injection: You can pick exactly which slots in your workhorse register are kept and which are replaced by values from the original register using an 8-bit immediate mask.
